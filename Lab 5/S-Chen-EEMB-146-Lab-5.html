A levene test was done on the transformed dandelion data in which the null hypothesis (H_0) is that the log variances are equal and the alternative hypothesis (H_A) is that the log variances are not equal. Because the test produced a p-value of 0.04464, which is less than 0.05, I reject the null hypothesis that the variances are equal.

Since the log variances are not equal I decided to do a Welch’s t-test on the transformed data. I’m choosing to use this instead of the Mann-Whitney U-Test because the Welch’s t-test is a parametric test and therefore is more powerful than the Mann-Whitney U-test.

# two sample t-test

1C

In this Welch’s t-test, the null hypothesis is that there is no difference in the log number of dandelion leaves per rosette between 0m from the habitat edge and 6m from the habitat edge (H_0: μ_1 = μ_2). The alternative hypothesis is that there is a difference in the log number of leaves per rosette between 0m from the edge and 6m from the edge (H_A: μ_1 =/= μ_2). A levene test was done on the transformed cricket data in which the null hypothesis (H_0) is that the sqrt variances are equal and the alternative hypothesis (H_A) is that the sqrt variances are not equal. This p-value of the levene test tells me that the chance of getting an F-score of 5.1746 by chance is 3.3%/ Because the test produced a p-value of 0.03302, which is less than 0.05, I reject the null hypothesis that the sqrt variances are equal.

Since the sqrt variances are not equal I decided to do a Welch’s t-test on the transformed data. I’m choosing to use this instead of the Mann-Whitney U-Test because the Welch’s t-test is a parametric test and therefore is more powerful than the Mann-Whitney U-test.

# two sample t-test
feeding_fed <- subset(cricket_data, feeding == "fed")
